# Reflection on the "GenAI-powered App-DB Modernization" Workshop

## Event Overview

The workshop on May 30 focused on application and database modernization in the age of GenAI. Instead of only introducing technical tools, the event connected several important topics: systems thinking, DevOps culture, cloud learning, psychological barriers, and practical product-building through hackathon-style work.

The main message I received from the event was that building technology products is not only about choosing the right AWS service or automation tool. A successful project also depends on communication, ownership, feedback loops, and the ability to keep moving even when the first version is not perfect.

## Main Topics

### Mindset, People, and Technology

One point that stood out to me was the way the presentations combined technical knowledge with human factors. The event did not treat problems such as missed deadlines, production bugs, poor collaboration, or procrastination as isolated issues. Instead, these problems were explained as symptoms of deeper causes.

For example, in project work, visible problems may come from weak communication, separated teams, manual processes, or unclear responsibility. In personal development, procrastination may not simply come from laziness, but from fear of failure, fear of judgment, or lack of confidence.

### DevOps Culture

The DevOps section helped me understand that DevOps is not only a collection of tools. The core of DevOps is the balance between **People, Process, and Technology**.

Automation can improve deployment speed and reduce manual mistakes, but it cannot replace trust, communication, and accountability inside a team. This idea is important because many technical problems actually start from workflow and collaboration problems.

### Cloud Learning and Cost Control

The event also discussed how beginners can learn AWS more safely. A common concern when practicing cloud services is unexpected billing, especially when resources are not deleted after testing.

To reduce that risk, the speakers introduced learning methods such as **AWS Cloud Quest** and local simulation with **Floci**. Floci was presented as a lightweight open-source AWS emulator that can help test cloud architecture locally with less cost and faster feedback.

### Practical Product Building

Another valuable topic was the "build fast, fail fast" mindset. Through examples from hackathon environments, I saw how teams can move quickly by focusing on the core problem, building a usable version first, receiving feedback, and improving the product step by step.

The event mentioned practical ideas such as **SynthHunter**, which helps distinguish AI-generated voices from real human voices to reduce fraud risks, and **Vortex**, an application that supports the recruitment process. These examples showed that strong ideas usually come from solving real problems.

## Technical Knowledge Gained

### Application Design and Modernization

I learned more about how modern systems can be analyzed and redesigned through practices such as **event storming**. This approach helps convert business processes into domain events, making it easier to understand how different parts of a system interact.

The workshop also introduced concepts such as **bounded contexts**, **microservice splitting**, and communication patterns. I became more aware of the trade-offs between synchronous and asynchronous communication, as well as integration styles such as pub/sub, point-to-point messaging, and streaming.

### GenAI in the Software Development Lifecycle

The event introduced **Amazon Q Developer** as a tool that can support different stages of the software development lifecycle, from planning and coding to modernization and maintenance.

I also learned how AI-assisted tools can help with code transformation and how serverless services such as **AWS Lambda** can support faster experimentation and deployment.

## Personal Takeaways

### Overcoming Psychological Barriers

A meaningful lesson from the event was that 70-80% of procrastination can come from difficulty managing emotions rather than laziness. Fear of not being good enough, fear of being judged, and fear of failure can prevent people from starting work.

Two simple methods that I found practical were the **5-Minute Rule** and the **5-Second Rule**. The 5-Minute Rule encourages starting a difficult task for just five minutes to create momentum. The 5-Second Rule uses a short countdown to push the body into action before hesitation takes over.

### Applying the Lessons to My Work

After the workshop, I think there are several ideas I can apply to my own learning and project work:

- Build stronger team workflows by encouraging shared responsibility and reducing blame.
- Create faster feedback loops so problems can be discovered early.
- Name my fears clearly instead of simply saying that I am lazy.
- Use small daily progress to build confidence.
- Practice AWS through safer methods such as AWS Cloud Quest and local simulation tools.
- Keep a "go build" mindset by making a usable version first, then improving it through feedback.

## Event Experience

Overall, the workshop gave me a broader view of how technology, workflow, and psychology are connected. I not only gained technical knowledge about cloud modernization, microservices, GenAI tools, and serverless development, but also learned how mindset and team culture affect the success of a project.

The most important thing I took away is that good engineering is not only about writing code. It also requires clear communication, responsibility, experimentation, and the courage to start before everything feels perfect.
